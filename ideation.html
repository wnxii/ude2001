Ideation

How our team brainstormed and refined ideas to create a unique solution for our project.

Requirement Gathering

User research:

To initiate our project, we engaged in structured requirement gathering to ensure alignment with real user needs and stakeholder expectations. This phase involved collaboration with:

  • Glasgow City Council (policy alignment, biodiversity goals)
  • Conservation organisations (e.g. Friends of Glasgow Green, RSPB Scotland)
  • Academic experts (urban ecology, HCI, AI ethics)

To help us analyze environments, activities, and user interactions during field observation and community walks, we utilised the AEIOU framework.

Activities:

Observing how citizens and experts interact with the species identification process, including reporting sightings and verifying data.

Environments:

Locations such as the Glasgow Botanic Gardens, community spaces, and digital platforms used for reporting and identification.

Interactions:

Communication between citizens, experts, and the reporting system, including email exchanges and feedback loops.

Objects:

Tools and resources used, such as cameras, smartphones, spreadsheets, and the iNaturalist platform.

Users:

Citizens reporting sightings, experts verifying species, and council staff managing biodiversity data.

Partner Meeting:

A partner meeting with our project partner, Chrissy, was conducted to gather insights and requirements for the project.

Chrissy is part of the Friends of Glasgow Botanic Gardens, a volunteer-led charitable organization that supports the conservation, enhancement, and public engagement efforts of the Glasgow Botanic Gardens.

Prior to the meeting, the team utlised the "How Might We" framework to better understand the situation and prepare some questions to ask Chrissy. Utilising this framework helped us avoid assumptions and focus interviews around real-world challenges. Some examples include:

?

How might we support experts in validating species data more efficiently?

?

How might we help users record accurate species details without making the process overwhelming?

?

How might we reduce errors in manual species data entry and improve consistency?

With these "How Might We" questions in mind, the table below summarizes these questions and their relevance to the project.

What is the current process of the species identification in Glasgow?

Currently, a single expert is responsible for the identification and recording of the species. Citizens would report observations of species through email, with the image of the species attached. The expert will then manually identify the species in the image, and then record it into a spreadsheet.

What is the relevance of iNaturalist with regards to our project?

It would be good to explore how iNaturalist data can be integrated into the current process such that there is one centralised database for the species. In addition to that, requests that come in through current process could be automatically routed to iNaturalist.

Key Insights:

Following the meeting, the team was able to gather key insights that would help shape the direction of the project. The key insights are summarised below:

1

The current process of identifying species in Glasgow is manual and time-consuming, with a single expert responsible for the identification and recording of species.

2

There is potential to integrate iNaturalist data into the current process to create a centralised database for species identification.

3

Requests that come in through the current process could be automatically routed to iNaturalist, streamlining the identification process.

Ideation Process

Initial Brainstorming phase:

Our ideation process was a collaborative effort that involved brainstorming, evaluating, and refining ideas to arrive at a unique solution. We utilized various techniques such as mind mapping, sketching, and group discussions to generate a wide range of concepts.

Each team member came up with many initial ideas, which was then placed onto a Action Priority Matrix, as seen in the embedded Miro Board, to determine the most feasible and impactful concepts. The ideas were grouped into four categories, that being:

1 Quick Wins (Top Left, Low Effort/High Impact)
2 Big Projects (Top Right, High Effort/High Impact)
3 Fill-Ins (Bottom Left, Low Effort/Low Impact)
4 Time Sinks (Bottom Right, High Effort/Low Impact)
Narrowing down ideas:

After brainstorming with a large pool of ideas, we narrowed down our focus to the projects that aligned with our goals and objectives. In addition, we considered the feasibility and effort for each idea, ultimately selected four ideas that stood out.

The projects that we selected mainly had high impact, and are outlined by the green and orange rectangles in the Miro Board.

The idea that is outlined by the orange rectangles was one that we considered. However, after considering the timeframe that the team had to complete the project, we decided to not pursue this idea as it would require too much effort within the timeframe.

Final Concept:

The final concept that we decided to pursue was a combination of the ideas that were had the highest impact and most feasible for the team to complete. These ideas were:

📋

An API to feed authenticated biodiversity data to the Glasgow City Council's reporting system

🕸️

A web portal for experts to rapidly verify, comment on, or correct flagged iNaturalist submissions

✅

A "triage" system that auto-verified high-confidence sightings of common species, leaving only challenging IDs for experts
