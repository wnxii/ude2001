Lew Jiajun

Going through this eventful journey from requirement gathering to solution designing has taught me various lessons and enhanced my views and modus operandi. It has taught me insightful lessons such as: how to ask good questions through the ‚Äúmum test‚Äù, handling conflicts during ideation by integrating them, how everybody has different mental models and the risks, benefits and how‚Äôs of aligning our mental models, the importance of integrating user feedback into our design process and many more lessons among those.

One memorable learning experience was when I was heavily involved with the ideation and prototyping process of this project. After finishing my segment, I simply handed over to the person in charge of the next step of the project and took my hands off of it. However, due to my negligence, the intended solution started diverging from the intended solution.

To remedy this, the team realigned our views of the solution by consolidating our diverging views onto a thinking model for a shared mental model throughout the team. This experience taught me the valuable lesson of keeping my involvement to ensure that my team members are on the same page and pursue a unified solution. The stages involved in designing a solution should not be silo but a constant collaborative journey.

This project has taught me numerous valuable lessons that will stick with me and shape how I approach future collaborative work. I‚Äôve learnt that good design isn‚Äôt just about having strong individual ideas but also co-creating with others, aligning perspectives, and continuously validating assumptions with real users. Moving forward, I‚Äôll be more intentional about maintaining open communication across stages, staying engaged beyond my assigned tasks, and embracing tension and feedback as opportunities for growth. These takeaways have not only enhanced my design thinking skills but also made me a more reflective and adaptive team member.

Deston Seet

Over the course of this project, I had the opportunity to work on every aspects of the project, from the ideation phase all the way to the final documentation of the proposed solution. I would say the journey had been interesting and fulfilling with many ups and downs.

The journey started at the ideation and problem statement clarification. At that stage, we were trying to figure out the details of the problem, gain context knowledge as well as brainstorm some potential ideas and solutions. This ideation phase was filled with back and forth conversation, clarifying each other‚Äôs understanding of the problem as well as the objective that we are specifically trying to solve. Throwing ideas around helped us find the best features that will solve the pain points of the problem. Looking back, I personally think that the mood of the discussion was the most important aspect. The right mood among my team members allowed everyone to contribute their thoughts and ideas freely without repercussions or judgments. The final solution was a combination of everyone‚Äôs inputs and apprehension, which fully satisfy the objective we laid out at the start of the ideation process. Therefore, I am glad that we, as a group, jelled well together and maintained a professional yet friendly atmosphere within the group, striving to reach the best idea possible.

The next major phase is the prototyping phase, which involves iterating through the prototype as it goes from lo-fi, mid-fi and high-fi. As it iterates, the word journey fits it really well. The process feels like a journey where it progressively builds on the past work, taking in different kind of feedback along the way in order to become a final prototype that reflects the final product. My takeaway is exactly that it was more of a journey than anything else and should be seen as 1 whole process. One of the key event during this process was that we tried to hasten the process by doing the mid-fi and high-fi simultaneously, skipping user feedback and proceeding with what we think was right. This came to haunt us as we realised that we missed many issues that could have been identified during the mid-fi stage. We learnt that there are no shortcut on the road to quality. I used to see prototyping as 3 different phases but after this experience, I see them as a long process that build ontop of each other to reach a quality product at the end.

While this project is coming to an end, the takeaways will stay with me in my future works and it had been an pleasant experience doing this project in Glasgow.

Hannah Woo

Working on the Figma prototype for the species identification website was a valuable exercise in both design precision and empathy. I had to pay close attention to detail,not just in terms of layout, colors, and consistency, but also in how the interface would actually be used by real experts in the field. The experts had an important task on verifying several species types in sometimes subtle visual differences in photos, so it was crucial to provide a seamless and smooth experience.

Designing for that kind of workflow meant stepping into their shoes and imagining what would make their job easier and more efficient. I had to think about how images are displayed, how information is prioritized, and how easy it is to compare, tag, or reject submissions. The goal was to reduce cognitive load and friction in the process, without sacrificing clarity or scientific accuracy. It challenged me to design not just something that looked good, but something that supports critical, detail-oriented work.

Designing the Figma prototype for the species identification website pushed me to think deeply about creating a visually appealing, seamless, and intuitive user experience. Throughout the design process, I found myself constantly evaluating decisions against Nielsen's usability heuristics, particularly focusing on how each choice would impact the mental workload of researchers engaged in detailed-oriented, repetitive identification tasks.

I became increasingly aware of how much small, thoughtful choices matter. For example, emphasis on consistency in spacing, type hierarchy, and color usage to ensure a clean visual rhythm. Rather than drawing attention to the design itself, I aimed to let the content and the task at hand stay front and center. It was a balance of form and function, where aesthetics serve usability. Seeing the pieces slowly but surely come together in a way that feels natural and smooth was definitely one of the most satisfying parts of the process.

Moreover, another complex challenge I faced was designing for such a varied group of users, not just because of their varying levels of expertise, but because each group accessed the system through different entry points and needed different functionalities. This pushed me to think very critically about user journeys. This meant that I had to put myself in the various users‚Äô shoes and gain insights on their expectations as well as their pain points, which the interviews and discussions conducted helped tremendously. It definitely was a little frustrating having to go through countless iterations to align with what the entire team wanted but the innate passion to solve the problem deeply motivated me. I wanted to create tailored and curated experiences that also felt cohesive across the entire platform. Isaiah Ang

Over the past three weeks, our team collaborated on a project aimed at streamlining the current process of species identification. Reflecting on the work we produced, we are proud of what we have managed to achieve within this short time frame. However, there are also many areas where we could have performed better as a team. Overall, the experience was deeply insightful and helped us to understand how we could work better as a team in future projects.

ü§ù

What we are proud of

One strength we recognised was our team‚Äôs willingness to adapt, especially when we didn‚Äôt initially agree on a particular approach. Whilst the team was in the process of creating the prototype, we had differing views on the layout of the page where the expert would verify the identification of the species.

Initially, our layout did not account for the expert to ‚Äúdisagree‚Äù with the prediction of the model. One of our team members pointed out this concern, leading on a discussion on whether or not to include a separate disagree button for the AI model prediction, and the justification for each. While maintaining a single button would be simpler, and the disagree button would rely on the iNaturalist‚Äôs identification and train the model in the background, having a separate button would allow the expert to provide direct feedback to our model.

We opted to have separate disagree buttons for the model and iNaturalist, as it improved the clarity and functionality of the expert input process. As such, with this, even though we had disagreements, we weighed different perspectives and decided on the option that gave the most value.

‚ö°

What we are not proud of

While there were many things we were proud of, we regretted not properly testing some of our initial ideas before implementing them. This led to design decisions that had to be reworked, affecting both our timeline and confidence in the solution.

During the early prototyping stages, we lacked user and team feedback. After completing our low-fidelity (Lo-Fi) prototype, we did not gather any feedback for our design, and moved straight into developing the mid-fidelity (Mid-Fi) prototype. The same problem continued with the Mid-Fi prototype, with us not consulting users or prompting any discussion within our team on any potential improvements.

When we developed the high-fidelity (Hi-Fi), then only did we seek user feedback. The feedback given revealed many core design issues that had not been addressed. This led to multiple iterations of the prototype, which led to a series of delays and difficult changes due to the time-consuming and complex nature of Hi-Fi prototype revision. This could have been avoided had we sought user feedback in all steps of the prototyping stages.

This experience taught us the importance of user testing and feedback at every stage of the design process. In future projects, we will prioritise this early, ensuring that user feedback is gotten in the early stages to ensure that the prototype is going in the right direction.

Conclusion

In conclusion, despite the obstacles that we faced throughout the project, the experiences gained was a valuable opportunity for growth both individually and as a team. We learnt the importance of early and consistent feedback, and the need to be adaptable when changes arise. We enjoyed the process doing this project, and value the insights gained.

Asking better questions with a Shared mental model using AEIOU

Our team's project started with a critical misalignment in our understanding of the problem. We each interpreted the problem statement differently, leading to confusion and a lack of clear direction. From this, we learnt that a shared understanding isn't guaranteed and is instead a deliberate, active, and explicit process.

The initial disconnect came from the use of iNaturalist within our solution. Team members were not sure if our partner needed an app like iNaturalist or to integrate iNaturalist into the solution. This was one of many confusions about the premise of the problem. Due to the open-ended nature of the problem statement that lacked specific constraints, there were a broad range of interpretations about the premise and context of the problem. There were many questions that we wanted to ask, but we were unsure if our questions were able to target all the different user needs and perspectives. We learnt that when tackling an open-ended problem, there are too many viewpoints to take into consideration and we needed a way to consolidate them.

To fix this, we used the AEIOU method to systematically map out our thoughts and created a shared mental model. It allowed us to consider things from the point of view of: activities, environment interactions, objects and users, which gave us a comprehensive overview of gaps in our questions. This allowed the questions we crafted to have a more holistic view to uncover various users and their needs. We learnt the importance of having a shared mental model within our group to align our goals and the importance of charting the different viewpoints involved with the problem.

One particularly memorable session involved shadowing an expert botanist during their routine survey work. Observing their workflow, noting patterns, and understanding the cognitive load involved in species identification completely reshaped our approach to interface design. We realized our initial designs were far too complex for field use.

The interviews also revealed the emotional aspects of citizen science participation. Users weren't just interested in data collection; they wanted to feel part of a larger conservation effort. This insight led us to incorporate progress indicators and community engagement features that made users feel their contributions were meaningful and impactful.

Perhaps most importantly, these sessions taught us to always question our assumptions. Every preconceived notion we had about user behavior was challenged, and often proven wrong. This experience has made me a much more humble and curious designer, always eager to learn from real users rather than rely on intuition.

Resolving ideation disagreements using integrative thinking

During our ideation phase, there was a divide within our group about the use of AI to automate the process as opposed to a human expert identifier. Our partner in the project made it clear that while AI can be helpful, it may be unreliable and so she discourages the use of it. This brought to light two opposing perspectives: one advocating for AI-led identification to leverage speed and scalability, and the other emphasizing the importance of expert judgement to ensure accuracy and trustworthiness. This clash of ideas forced us to grapple with the limitations and strengths of both approaches, having to choose one or the other.

To move forward, we applied integrative thinking. Rather than choosing one side, we faced the tension of opposing ideas to generate a creative solution that contains elements of the opposing ideas but is superior to simply using one. We identified the pros of each idea and identified the central tension of ‚ÄúHow can we use both AI and human expertise without compromising reliability or efficiency?‚Äù

Our breakthrough came when we proposed a system where the AI suggests a species, but the expert makes the final call. This approach allowed us to retain the reliability of the expert, while still benefiting from AI‚Äôs speed and consistency. By embracing the tension instead of avoiding it, we created a more resilient and well-rounded solution.

This experience taught us several important lessons. First, conflicting ideas are not roadblocks but opportunities for creative resolution. Disagreements can lead to stronger, more innovative outcomes when explored thoughtfully. Second, decision-making models such as integrative thinking help push us beyond surface-level comparisons. They encourage deeper analysis and structured reasoning, which ultimately guided our team to a consensus. Lastly, we learnt that limitations like AI‚Äôs occasional inaccuracy can be reframed as creative constraints that drive better design. In our case, AI‚Äôs weakness of unreliability was mitigated when it was complemented with expert review to give the final verdict.

When we encounter future opposing idea challenges, we no longer view weaknesses and constraints as limitations to our solution but a springboard to innovation. With integrative thinking, we can use structured thinking models to explore deep thinking avenues that guide us toward integrating our two opposing ideas into a stronger and more resilient solution.

User feedback - Testing should be done continuously

A key theme that emerged during this phase of our project was the importance of continuous user feedback in shaping a user-centered product. When we sought feedback on our high-fidelity prototypes, it quickly became apparent that our designs had various usability gaps. One screen, for example, only had a single ‚Äúagree‚Äù button without clearly indicating which suggestion it referred to. This ambiguity could have been easily caught earlier, had we involved users consistently throughout.

Reflecting on this, we recognized a major oversight in our process: we had skipped user feedback during the transition from mid-fi to high-fi, assuming that our design choices were sufficient. This complacency led us to rely on internal assumptions rather than real user needs. As a result, what seemed like obvious flaws in hindsight were completely missed until much later.

This experience taught us that user feedback is not a one-time checkbox, but a vital and ongoing part of the design process. By neglecting it, we risked drifting away from user needs and investing time in refining ideas that did not fully capture the essence of our solution. In future projects, we are committed to embedding user testing into every stage, not just to validate our work, but to inform continuously. This approach will help us catch issues earlier, adapt more effectively, and stay aligned with real-world use cases.

We learned to observe not just what users did, but how they felt doing it. Facial expressions, sighs of frustration, and moments of confusion were as valuable as click data. One participant's comment - "I feel stupid using this" - completely changed how we approached error messaging and help text. We realized that poor usability doesn't just waste time; it makes people feel bad about themselves.

Ultimately, this experience deepened our understanding of user feedback in design as a collaborative and iterative process. It reminded us that the best products are not designed in isolation, but in conversation with the people they are meant to serve.

Edge cases and disparity between developers and users

Another key learning point in our design journey emerged from continued user testing, specifically, how real user behaviour can challenge our initial assumptions. For the species identification feature, we initially designed a multi-step process: users would upload a photo, receive multiple AI-generated species suggestions based on confidence levels, and then select one to proceed to an information page. We believed that this would empower users to make more accurate selections and feel more in control.

However, user feedback revealed that this assumption was flawed. Users were not experts and in practice, most users simply selected the first option without evaluating the others, undermining the purpose of the feature. What we had thought was a helpful layer of precision ended up introducing friction and unnecessarily complicating the experience.

In response, we removed the species suggestion step entirely and streamlined the user flow to lead directly from photo upload to the animal‚Äôs information page. This change better matched user expectations and behaviour, reducing confusion and making the process more efficient.

This experience reinforced a critical insight: our assumptions, no matter how logical, must be validated through actual user testing. There‚Äôs often a gulf between how designers imagine a feature will be used and how users actually interact with it. Assumptions should not be treated as truths, but as hypotheses that need testing.

User testing also helped us uncover edge cases and overlooked scenarios, particularly from the expert‚Äôs perspective. We assumed that the expert would want to log every image sourced from iNaturalist, believing that all data is valuable. However, feedback highlighted that some images might be of poor quality or irrelevant, making them unsuitable for logging. Yet, our interface had no option for the expert to skip or ignore such entries, an oversight on our part.

This revealed the importance of designing for flexibility and real-world context, not just the ideal use case. By failing to consider exceptions, we risked frustrating users and forcing them into decisions that didn‚Äôt align with their goals.

Overall, these experiences have taught us that user testing is not only about validation but also discovery. It reveals gaps, surfaces unexpected needs, and pushes us to let go of assumptions in favour of real user needs. Moving forward, we are committed to grounding our design decisions in actual user behaviour, not just theoretical logic, and ensuring that our solutions are shaped by the people who use them.
