Personal Growth

Throughout this project, I've experienced significant growth in both technical skills and collaborative abilities. Working on Botaniq challenged me to think beyond traditional UX patterns and consider the unique needs of citizen scientists and environmental researchers.

The most valuable lesson was learning to balance user needs with technical constraints. Initially, I focused heavily on feature-rich interfaces, but user testing revealed that simplicity and clarity were far more important for our target audience.

One of the most challenging aspects was learning to let go of preconceived notions about what users wanted. I had to develop humility and accept that my assumptions were often wrong. This shift in mindset was uncomfortable at first but ultimately liberating.

The project also taught me the importance of cross-functional collaboration. Working closely with developers, researchers, and domain experts expanded my perspective and improved the quality of our solutions. I learned to communicate design decisions more effectively and to incorporate technical constraints into my design process from the beginning rather than treating them as afterthoughts.

Perhaps most importantly, I developed confidence in my ability to tackle complex, ambiguous problems. The Botaniq project had no clear precedent, which forced me to think creatively and develop novel approaches. This experience has prepared me to take on more challenging design problems in the future.

Skills Development

This project significantly enhanced my prototyping and user research skills. Learning to conduct effective user interviews and synthesize findings into actionable design decisions was particularly valuable.

I also developed a deeper appreciation for accessibility and inclusive design. Designing for diverse user groups, including those with varying levels of botanical knowledge, taught me the importance of progressive disclosure and contextual help.

The research phase was particularly transformative. I learned to ask better questions, to listen more carefully, and to identify patterns across different user interviews. The process of creating personas and user journey maps became much more meaningful when grounded in real user data rather than assumptions.

My prototyping skills evolved from creating static mockups to building interactive prototypes that could effectively communicate complex interactions. Learning to use tools like Figma more effectively and understanding when to use different fidelities of prototypes was crucial for getting meaningful feedback from stakeholders and users.

I also gained experience in facilitating design workshops and collaborative sessions. Learning to guide discussions, manage different personalities, and synthesize diverse viewpoints into actionable insights was a valuable skill that extends beyond design work.

Design Philosophy Evolution

My design philosophy has evolved from aesthetic-focused to user-centered. Before this project, I often prioritized visual appeal over functionality. Working on Botaniq taught me that great design is invisible ‚Äì it enables users to accomplish their goals without friction.

I've also learned to embrace iterative design. The multiple rounds of prototyping and testing showed me that the best solutions often emerge through refinement rather than initial brilliance. Each iteration brought us closer to a solution that truly serves our users.

This shift required unlearning some habits I had developed. I used to become attached to my first ideas and defend them vigorously. Now I understand that design is about solving problems, not showcasing creativity. The best designs often feel obvious in hindsight, which means they're doing their job well.

I've also developed a deeper appreciation for the entire user experience, not just the interface. Understanding the context in which people use our product, their motivations, frustrations, and goals has become central to how I approach design problems. This holistic view has made me a better collaborator with product managers, developers, and other stakeholders.

The concept of "designing for the 80%" versus "designing for edge cases" became much clearer through this project. Learning when to optimize for the majority use case and when to accommodate outliers required careful consideration of user research data and business priorities.

Moving forward, I'm committed to maintaining this user-centered approach while continuing to develop my aesthetic sensibilities. The goal is to create designs that are both beautiful and functional, where visual appeal serves to enhance usability rather than distract from it.

Asking better questions with a Shared mental model using AEIOU

Our team's project started with a critical misalignment in our understanding of the problem. We each interpreted the problem statement differently, leading to confusion and a lack of clear direction. From this, we learnt that a shared understanding isn't guaranteed and is instead a deliberate, active, and explicit process.

The initial disconnect came from the use of iNaturalist within our solution. Team members were not sure if our partner needed an app like iNaturalist or to integrate iNaturalist into the solution. This was one of many confusions about the premise of the problem. Due to the open-ended nature of the problem statement that lacked specific constraints, there were a broad range of interpretations about the premise and context of the problem. There were many questions that we wanted to ask, but we were unsure if our questions were able to target all the different user needs and perspectives. We learnt that when tackling an open-ended problem, there are too many viewpoints to take into consideration and we needed a way to consolidate them.

To fix this, we used the AEIOU method to systematically map out our thoughts and created a shared mental model. It allowed us to consider things from the point of view of: activities, environment interactions, objects and users, which gave us a comprehensive overview of gaps in our questions. This allowed the questions we crafted to have a more holistic view to uncover various users and their needs. We learnt the importance of having a shared mental model within our group to align our goals and the importance of charting the different viewpoints involved with the problem.

One particularly memorable session involved shadowing an expert botanist during their routine survey work. Observing their workflow, noting patterns, and understanding the cognitive load involved in species identification completely reshaped our approach to interface design. We realized our initial designs were far too complex for field use.

The interviews also revealed the emotional aspects of citizen science participation. Users weren't just interested in data collection; they wanted to feel part of a larger conservation effort. This insight led us to incorporate progress indicators and community engagement features that made users feel their contributions were meaningful and impactful.

Perhaps most importantly, these sessions taught us to always question our assumptions. Every preconceived notion we had about user behavior was challenged, and often proven wrong. This experience has made me a much more humble and curious designer, always eager to learn from real users rather than rely on intuition.

Resolving ideation disagreements using integrative thinking

During our ideation phase, there was a divide within our group about the use of AI to automate the process as opposed to a human expert identifier. Our partner in the project made it clear that while AI can be helpful, it may be unreliable and so she discourages the use of it. This brought to light two opposing perspectives: one advocating for AI-led identification to leverage speed and scalability, and the other emphasizing the importance of expert judgement to ensure accuracy and trustworthiness. This clash of ideas forced us to grapple with the limitations and strengths of both approaches, having to choose one or the other.

To move forward, we applied integrative thinking. Rather than choosing one side, we faced the tension of opposing ideas to generate a creative solution that contains elements of the opposing ideas but is superior to simply using one. We identified the pros of each idea and identified the central tension of ‚ÄúHow can we use both AI and human expertise without compromising reliability or efficiency?‚Äù

Our breakthrough came when we proposed a system where the AI suggests a species, but the expert makes the final call. This approach allowed us to retain the reliability of the expert, while still benefiting from AI‚Äôs speed and consistency. By embracing the tension instead of avoiding it, we created a more resilient and well-rounded solution.

This experience taught us several important lessons. First, conflicting ideas are not roadblocks but opportunities for creative resolution. Disagreements can lead to stronger, more innovative outcomes when explored thoughtfully. Second, decision-making models such as integrative thinking help push us beyond surface-level comparisons. They encourage deeper analysis and structured reasoning, which ultimately guided our team to a consensus. Lastly, we learnt that limitations like AI‚Äôs occasional inaccuracy can be reframed as creative constraints that drive better design. In our case, AI‚Äôs weakness of unreliability was mitigated when it was complemented with expert review to give the final verdict.

When we encounter future opposing idea challenges, we no longer view weaknesses and constraints as limitations to our solution but a springboard to innovation. With integrative thinking, we can use structured thinking models to explore deep thinking avenues that guide us toward integrating our two opposing ideas into a stronger and more resilient solution.

User feedback - Testing should be done continuously

A key theme that emerged during this phase of our project was the importance of continuous user feedback in shaping a user-centered product. When we sought feedback on our high-fidelity prototypes, it quickly became apparent that our designs had various usability gaps. One screen, for example, only had a single ‚Äúagree‚Äù button without clearly indicating which suggestion it referred to. This ambiguity could have been easily caught earlier, had we involved users consistently throughout.

Reflecting on this, we recognized a major oversight in our process: we had skipped user feedback during the transition from mid-fi to high-fi, assuming that our design choices were sufficient. This complacency led us to rely on internal assumptions rather than real user needs. As a result, what seemed like obvious flaws in hindsight were completely missed until much later.

This experience taught us that user feedback is not a one-time checkbox, but a vital and ongoing part of the design process. By neglecting it, we risked drifting away from user needs and investing time in refining ideas that did not fully capture the essence of our solution. In future projects, we are committed to embedding user testing into every stage, not just to validate our work, but to inform continuously. This approach will help us catch issues earlier, adapt more effectively, and stay aligned with real-world use cases.

We learned to observe not just what users did, but how they felt doing it. Facial expressions, sighs of frustration, and moments of confusion were as valuable as click data. One participant's comment - "I feel stupid using this" - completely changed how we approached error messaging and help text. We realized that poor usability doesn't just waste time; it makes people feel bad about themselves.

Ultimately, this experience deepened our understanding of user feedback in design as a collaborative and iterative process. It reminded us that the best products are not designed in isolation, but in conversation with the people they are meant to serve.

Edge cases and disparity between developers and users

Another key learning point in our design journey emerged from continued user testing, specifically, how real user behaviour can challenge our initial assumptions. For the species identification feature, we initially designed a multi-step process: users would upload a photo, receive multiple AI-generated species suggestions based on confidence levels, and then select one to proceed to an information page. We believed that this would empower users to make more accurate selections and feel more in control.

However, user feedback revealed that this assumption was flawed. Users were not experts and in practice, most users simply selected the first option without evaluating the others, undermining the purpose of the feature. What we had thought was a helpful layer of precision ended up introducing friction and unnecessarily complicating the experience.

In response, we removed the species suggestion step entirely and streamlined the user flow to lead directly from photo upload to the animal‚Äôs information page. This change better matched user expectations and behaviour, reducing confusion and making the process more efficient.

This experience reinforced a critical insight: our assumptions, no matter how logical, must be validated through actual user testing. There‚Äôs often a gulf between how designers imagine a feature will be used and how users actually interact with it. Assumptions should not be treated as truths, but as hypotheses that need testing.

User testing also helped us uncover edge cases and overlooked scenarios, particularly from the expert‚Äôs perspective. We assumed that the expert would want to log every image sourced from iNaturalist, believing that all data is valuable. However, feedback highlighted that some images might be of poor quality or irrelevant, making them unsuitable for logging. Yet, our interface had no option for the expert to skip or ignore such entries, an oversight on our part.

This revealed the importance of designing for flexibility and real-world context, not just the ideal use case. By failing to consider exceptions, we risked frustrating users and forcing them into decisions that didn‚Äôt align with their goals.

Overall, these experiences have taught us that user testing is not only about validation but also discovery. It reveals gaps, surfaces unexpected needs, and pushes us to let go of assumptions in favour of real user needs. Moving forward, we are committed to grounding our design decisions in actual user behaviour, not just theoretical logic, and ensuring that our solutions are shaped by the people who use them.
